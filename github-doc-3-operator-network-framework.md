# Building a Verified Global Freight Operator Network
## A Field Research Framework

**Published by:** Faruk Olawale Quadri, Co-Founder and COO, Circuit 40s Ltd
**Last updated:** May 2026

---

## Overview

This document describes the framework Circuit 40s uses to identify, verify, and onboard logistics operators into its global freight network. It is published as a contribution to the research community working on freight technology, operator networks, and global trade infrastructure.

Building a credible operator network in logistics requires a different approach to most digital marketplace models. Operators handle physical cargo, cross international borders, and operate under regulatory frameworks that vary significantly by country. A network that does not account for this complexity will fail in practice even if it works in theory.

This framework documents what we have learned from building an operator network spanning multiple continents.

---

## Why Operator Verification Matters

In digital freight coordination, the quality of the network depends entirely on the quality of the operators within it. An unverified operator who cannot deliver on their claimed capabilities does not just create a problem for one shipment. They create a liability for every party in the chain that depended on them.

Verification is not a compliance exercise. It is a quality standard. The goal is to ensure that every operator in the network can be trusted to execute their stage of a shipment reliably, handle documentation correctly, and communicate in a way that allows the rest of the chain to function.

This matters more in cross-border freight than in domestic logistics because the consequences of operator failure are amplified across time zones, regulatory environments, and physical distances that make real-time intervention difficult.

## Operator Categories

The Circuit 40s network includes operators across the following service categories. Each category has distinct verification requirements reflecting the regulatory and operational complexity of that service type.

**Freight Forwarding**
Operators who coordinate the movement of cargo across international borders, typically managing booking, documentation, and carrier relationships on behalf of shippers.

**Customs Brokerage and Clearance**
Licensed agents authorised to clear cargo through customs on behalf of importers and exporters. Verification for this category includes confirmation of customs authority licensing in the relevant jurisdiction.

**Trucking and Inland Transport**
Operators providing road freight services within a country or across land borders. Includes general cargo, temperature-controlled, dangerous goods, and oversized cargo specialists.

**Port and Terminal Handling**
Operators managing cargo at port facilities, including loading, unloading, container drayage, and storage within port environments.

**Air Freight Handling**
Operators providing freight forwarding and handling services for air cargo, including perishable, high-value, and express shipments.

**Warehousing and Storage**
Operators providing storage facilities including bonded warehouses, temperature-controlled storage, and fulfilment services.

**Last-Mile Delivery**
Operators providing final delivery from a distribution point to the end recipient, including urban delivery, rural delivery, and specialised delivery services.

## Geographic Coverage Principles

The network is built to cover the trade lanes and transit corridors through which international freight actually moves, rather than being structured around administrative regions. This distinction matters because freight does not respect political geography. A shipment moving from Asia to West Africa may transit through the Middle East or Europe depending on the mode of transport. The operator network must be capable of covering the actual path of a shipment, not just its origin and destination.

Coverage is assessed at the corridor level rather than the country level. An operator in a landlocked country who specialises in cross-border road freight on a specific corridor is more valuable to the network than a broad-coverage operator with shallow capabilities across multiple routes.

## Verification Framework

Operator verification is conducted across four dimensions.

**1. Legal and Regulatory Verification**
Confirmation that the operator is legally registered and holds the necessary licenses and certifications to operate in their stated service categories and geographies. This includes company registration documents, customs authority licenses where applicable, and any sector-specific certifications required by the regulatory environment.

**2. Operational Capability Verification**
Confirmation that the operator has the physical and operational capacity to deliver on their stated service offering. This is assessed through direct engagement rather than self-declaration, including questions about volume handled, equipment available, geographic reach, and handling capabilities for specialised cargo types.

**3. Communication and Documentation Standards**
Assessment of how the operator communicates during live shipments and how they handle documentation. Operators who rely entirely on informal communication channels and have no documentation practices are assessed as higher risk. This is not a disqualifying factor but informs how the operator is positioned within the network.

**4. Track Record and References**
Where available, evidence of the operator's track record is reviewed. This includes client references, volume history, and any publicly available information about their reputation in their local market.

## Onboarding Process

The onboarding process is structured to collect the verification data described above while establishing a working relationship with the operator.

**Initial outreach**
Operators are contacted through a standardised outreach process that explains the Circuit 40s network, the verification requirements, and what participation involves. The outreach is designed to be direct and respectful of the operator's time.

**Application and documentation submission**
Operators complete a structured application that captures company details, service categories, geographic coverage, licensing information, and operational capacity. Supporting documents are submitted alongside the application.

**Verification review**
Submitted information is reviewed against the four-dimension framework. Where information is incomplete or requires clarification, the operator is contacted directly.

**Network confirmation**
Operators who meet the verification standards are confirmed as verified members of the Circuit 40s network. They receive confirmation of their verified status and the service categories and geographies under which they are listed.

**Ongoing relationship**
Verification is not a one-time event. The operator relationship is maintained through ongoing communication, performance monitoring, and periodic re-verification as the network evolves.

## What We Have Learned

Building this network across multiple continents has produced several observations that are worth documenting for the benefit of others working in this space.

**Informal networks are the real infrastructure**
In many markets, freight is coordinated through relationships built over years rather than through formal systems. Operators who have worked together before have established trust that no platform can replicate immediately. Building a technology network on top of this reality requires understanding and respecting these existing relationships rather than trying to replace them.

**Documentation practices vary enormously**
What counts as adequate documentation for a shipment varies significantly by market, regulatory environment, and operator type. A verification framework that applies a single documentation standard across all markets will fail. Standards must be calibrated to what is realistic and enforceable in each context.

**Operator communication preferences are not uniform**
Different operators in different markets have strong preferences for how they communicate. Email is preferred in some markets. WhatsApp dominates in others. Phone calls remain the primary channel for urgent matters in most markets. A network that requires all operators to communicate through a single channel will face adoption resistance.

**Verification builds trust on both sides**
Operators who go through a rigorous verification process feel more valued and more committed to the network than those who are onboarded with minimal friction. Verification signals that the network takes quality seriously, which attracts operators who take their own work seriously.
