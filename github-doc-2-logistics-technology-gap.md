# The Coordination Gap in Global Freight
## An Industry Research Document on the Missing Layer in Logistics Technology

**Published by:** Faruk Olawale Quadri, Co-Founder and COO, Circuit 40s Ltd
**Last updated:** May 2026

---

## Overview

This document examines a structural gap in global logistics technology: the absence of a reliable coordination layer between the multiple independent operators involved in executing an international shipment. It draws on publicly available industry data, academic research, and field observations from direct engagement with logistics operators across multiple continents.

It is published here as a contribution to the broader research conversation about where logistics technology is failing and why.

---

## The Problem

International freight does not move through a single company. A shipment originating in one country and arriving in another typically passes through a chain of independent operators, each responsible for a specific stage of the journey. A freight forwarder books the cargo. A port handling company loads it. A shipping line carries it across an ocean. A customs agent clears it at the destination port. A trucking company moves it inland. A last-mile operator delivers it to the final recipient.

Each of these operators is independent. Each has their own systems, their own communication channels, and their own way of confirming that their stage is complete. And at each transition between operators, something must pass: documents, instructions, confirmation that the previous stage is done and the next one can begin.

This transition point is the coordination layer. And in most international shipments today, it does not exist in any structured digital form.

---

## What the Data Shows

The scale of the problem is significant and well documented.

The global logistics market is valued at approximately $5.9 trillion annually. Despite this scale, the industry's digital infrastructure remains fragmented at the operator level.

Research from the World Economic Forum estimates that inefficiencies in global supply chains cost the global economy over $600 billion annually. A significant proportion of these losses are attributable to coordination failures between operators rather than to physical delays in cargo movement.

Studies in logistics operations research have consistently identified documentation errors and inter-operator communication failures as among the top causes of shipment delays. The BCG Global Supply Chain report documented that documentation errors alone account for between 20 and 40 percent of supply chain disruption events.

Digitisation rates among freight operators remain low. Research indicates that fewer than one in four freight forwarders have digitised more than 75 percent of their processes. Among smaller operators, the figure is considerably lower.

Despite significant investment in freight technology platforms over the past decade, the focus has been predominantly on shipper-facing tools: rate comparison, booking, and tracking dashboards. The operator-to-operator coordination layer has received comparatively little attention.

---

## How Operators Currently Coordinate

Field research conducted across operators in multiple regions reveals a consistent pattern regardless of geography, company size, or service type.

**Communication channels**
The dominant communication tools used between operators during live shipments are WhatsApp and email. Phone calls are used for urgent matters. Formal system-to-system communication is rare outside of large enterprise operators.

**Document exchange**
Documents are exchanged via email attachment, WhatsApp, or physical handover at port facilities. There is no standard format for what documents must be provided at each stage transition, and no systematic confirmation that documents have been received and verified before the next stage begins.

**Status updates**
Shipment status is communicated reactively. Operators update their counterparties when something notable happens rather than at structured checkpoints. This means delays are often discovered at the point where they cause a problem rather than when they first arise.

**Confirmation of completion**
Each operator has their own method for confirming that their stage is complete. Some use signed delivery notes. Some send an email with attached documents. Some rely on verbal confirmation from a driver or recipient. There is no consistent, verifiable confirmation mechanism shared across the operator chain.

## Why Existing Technology Has Not Solved This

Several categories of freight technology address adjacent problems without solving the coordination gap.

**Freight marketplaces and booking platforms**
These connect shippers with carriers or freight forwarders at the point of booking. They do not address what happens between multiple operators during execution.

**Shipment tracking and visibility tools**
These provide real-time location data for cargo in transit. They address the question of where a shipment is, not the question of what information is passing between operators and whether handoffs are being executed correctly.

**TMS and ERP systems**
Transportation Management Systems and Enterprise Resource Planning tools help large operators manage their internal operations. They are not designed for coordination between independent operators who do not share systems.

**Digital freight forwarding platforms**
These digitise the freight forwarder's workflow but typically treat the forwarder as the single point of coordination. They do not address the coordination between the forwarder and the other independent operators in the chain.

The gap that remains is a shared coordination layer that sits between independent operators, standardises what information passes at each handoff, and provides both parties with confirmation that the transition has occurred correctly.

---

## The Implications for Freight Technology

The absence of this coordination layer has several downstream consequences.

**Accountability gaps**
When a shipment is delayed or damaged, identifying at which stage the problem occurred is difficult because there is no reliable record of what was handed over between operators and when. Disputes between operators are common and difficult to resolve.

**Documentation failures**
Mismatched or incomplete documentation at handoff points is one of the most frequent causes of customs delays and port holds. Without a structured handoff process, documentation errors are discovered reactively rather than prevented proactively.

**Scalability constraints**
Freight companies seeking to scale their operations face a coordination ceiling. Managing more shipments through informal communication channels becomes increasingly difficult as volume grows. The absence of structured coordination limits how efficiently operators can scale.

**Barriers to market entry**
New operators entering a market must establish informal communication relationships with each other party in the chain before they can operate effectively. The absence of a shared coordination standard makes it harder for new operators to integrate into existing freight networks.

---

## Conclusion

The logistics technology sector has made significant progress in digitising shipper-facing workflows, booking processes, and cargo visibility. The operator-to-operator coordination layer remains largely unaddressed.

Solving this problem requires building infrastructure that sits between independent operators, standardises information exchange at each handoff, and provides verifiable confirmation of stage completion. This is a software and systems design challenge as much as a logistics challenge, and it is one that the industry has not yet adequately addressed.
