# The Logistics Coordination Framework

**A public research framework documenting the coordination gap in logistics and the operator infrastructure layer needed to solve it.**

By Faruk Olawale Quadri, Co-Founder and COO, Circuit 40s Ltd

## The Problem

Every shipment, domestic or international, moves through multiple independent operators. A freight forwarder handles booking. A customs broker handles clearance. A trucking company moves the cargo inland. A warehouse holds it temporarily. A last-mile provider delivers to the final destination.

None of these operators share a common operating environment.

They coordinate over email, WhatsApp, phone calls, and spreadsheets. When one operator is slow to confirm, every operator downstream becomes stranded. Documents get lost in inboxes. The same information gets re-entered into five different systems by five different people.

The result is an information gap that costs the global logistics industry over $600 billion annually in bad data and fragmented communication. Supply chain disruptions cost organisations $184 billion every year. And yet the infrastructure layer that connects operators has never been properly built.

## The Coordination Gap Defined

The coordination gap is the absence of a shared digital environment between logistics operators executing stages of the same shipment.

It has three dimensions.

**1. Information fragmentation**
Each operator holds a piece of the shipment picture. No single party has full visibility of the end to end journey. Decisions are made with incomplete information.

**2. Manual handoffs**
When one operator completes their stage, the handoff to the next operator is triggered manually over email, phone call, or WhatsApp message. There is no automated sequencing. There is no structured confirmation. There is no audit trail.

**3. Document duplication**
The same document, whether a bill of lading, a customs declaration, or a packing list, is emailed to multiple parties, re-entered manually into different systems, and re-verified at every stage. Human error compounds at every handoff.


## Why This Has Not Been Solved

Logistics technology has focused primarily on two layers.

**Visibility tools** are platforms that track where a shipment is. These solve the symptom not the cause. A shipment being visible does not mean it is moving.

**Freight marketplaces** are platforms that connect shippers to carriers for rate discovery and booking. These solve the procurement layer not the execution layer.

Neither addresses the coordination layer, which is the infrastructure that sequences operators, manages handoffs between them, and gives every party shared visibility of the workflow in real time.

This is the missing layer.


## The Operator Network Model

Based on direct research and practitioner experience across domestic and international trade routes, an effective operator coordination framework requires the following components.

**Operator classification**
Operators must be categorised by service type, geographic coverage, capacity, and performance history. Classification enables intelligent matching of operators to shipment stages regardless of whether the shipment is domestic or cross-border.

**Stage sequencing**
Each shipment stage must be defined, sequenced, and assigned before execution begins. The sequence must be visible to all parties involved including freight forwarders, customs brokers, trucking companies, warehouse operators, and last-mile providers.

**Automated handoffs**
When an operator completes their stage, the handoff to the next operator must be triggered automatically without requiring manual confirmation from either party.

**Shared document environment**
All documents related to a shipment must be stored in a single environment accessible to every operator involved at the appropriate stage of the journey.

**Performance tracking**
Operator performance must be measured and recorded at every stage. Response time, completion time, and accuracy must be tracked to inform future operator selection.

## Operator Types This Framework Covers

The coordination gap affects every operator type involved in the movement of goods.

| Operator Type | Role in Shipment |
|---|---|
| Freight Forwarder | Booking, documentation, and route coordination |
| Customs Broker | Clearance, compliance, and regulatory documentation |
| Trucking Company | Inland cargo movement and drayage |
| Warehouse Operator | Temporary storage and cargo handling |
| Port Handler | Loading, unloading, and port side operations |
| Last-Mile Provider | Final delivery to destination |
| Air Freight Operator | Air cargo movement and handling |
| Ocean Freight Operator | Sea cargo movement and container management |

All of these operator types face the same coordination gap. All of them are affected by the same information fragmentation, manual handoffs, and document duplication.
